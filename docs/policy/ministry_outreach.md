**Document ID**: `policy/ministry_outreach`  
**Date**: 2026-05-13  
**Status**: Draft (pre-commitment)

## 0. Context & Rationale

Animetor Eval operates under explicit **labor-first framing** (STANCE.md §1) and a three-track impact model (publication 59% / policy 32% / business 9%). The ministry outreach pathway represents the **policy track's primary execution mechanism**, leveraging public research transfer to align data infrastructure with regulatory and strategic priorities across four government windows.

This protocol establishes:

1. **Ministry window identification** — staffing, decision authority, policy cycles
2. **Interest alignment** — what structural data each ministry needs
3. **Contact sequencing** — risk gradient and trust-building pathway
4. **Conflict-of-interest (COI) disclosure** — startup context and bias mitigation
5. **Report-to-window matching** — which analysis products serve which stakeholder

**Underlying principle**: Transparent about position (research + startup + labor-first framing), evidence-led (no advocacy-by-implication), and capacity-constrained (deliverables matched to realistic team bandwidth).

## 1. Four-Ministry Windows

### 1.1 METI (Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry)
#### Contact Industries Bureau, Content Industry Division (商務・サービスグループ コンテンツ産業課)

**Jurisdiction & Policy Cycles**:
- Cool Japan strategy (文化産業・コンテンツ戦略)
- Anime as priority export commodity (ポップカルチャー振興)
- Industry structure surveys (産業基盤調査事業)
- Animation production competitiveness assessment

**Typical staffing**:
- Course director (課長) or deputy director (課長補佐)
- Industry analysts (専門職員)
- Policy research commissioning authority (調査委託決定権)

**Policy cycles**:
- Cool Japan Strategy reviews (biennial, Q1–Q2)
- Subsidy program design (annual, Q3–Q4 planning)
- Industry survey RFPs (ongoing, Q2–Q3 launch typical)

**Assumed interest in Animetor Eval**:

1. **Production capacity metrics** — validated quantification of studio production scale (staff count × episodes × duration) without reliance on sales/budget data (proprietary). Currently, METI surveys rely on firm self-reporting; Animetor's credit-based alternative offers independent validation.

2. **Labor stability & retention** — "Next-year credit visibility loss rate" (翌年クレジット可視性喪失率) as proxy for workforce stability. Directly supports labor-intensive industry policy (retaining skilled animation staff = strategic priority for 国際競争力).

3. **Studio network topology** — collaboration density, repeated-partner ratios, new-entrant studio entry barriers. Relevant to "competitive market structure" assessments (METI's regulatory oversight role).

4. **Market concentration** — HHI-adjacent measures on production volume share, credit distribution inequality. Addresses METI's competition policy mandate.

**Assumed concerns**:
- IP sensitivity (producer/committee data not shareable; animation IP belongs to rights holders)
- Relevance to ongoing subsidy programs (will data feed into budget allocation?)
- Feasibility of regular data updates (pilot survey vs. operational intelligence)

**Recommended approach**:
- Emphasize **research transfer** frame (not consulting)
- Position as **independent validation** of existing METI surveys, not replacement
- Offer specific deliverables: 2-page brief on studio capacity distribution + production concentration (no proprietary studio financials revealed)
- Propose follow-up collaborative research with METI methodology team (co-authored survey design)

### 1.2 Ministry of Education, Culture, Sports, Science and Technology (MEXT / 文化庁)
#### Arts and Culture Division + Intellectual Property Strategy Bureau
*Contact via: Media Art Division (メディア芸術課) and Cultural Promotion Division (文化振興課)*

**Jurisdiction & Policy Cycles**:
- Cultural Content Promotion Act (文化芸術推進基本計画)
- Anime as cultural heritage & creative industry
- Artist welfare & labor standards in creative fields
- Cultural content export strategy (Cool Japan coordination with METI)

**Typical staffing**:
- Division director (課長) or deputy
- Cultural policy specialists
- Research coordination officers (if research promotion bureau involved)

**Policy cycles**:
- Cultural Promotion Plan reviews (5-year, next review 2027–2028 planning begins 2026 Q4)
- Artist welfare initiatives (ongoing; labor standards consultations 2025–2026)
- Media arts funding program design (annual, Q2–Q3)

**Assumed interest in Animetor Eval**:

1. **Labor rights & dignity in creative work** — Documented inequality in credit visibility, opportunity structures by demographic cohort (gender, entry year cohort, studio affiliation). Directly relevant to government's stated cultural policy goal: "尊厳のある文化人の労働環境" (dignified labor environment for cultural workers).

2. **Gender & opportunity disparity** — Quantified evidence of gender-differentiated role assignment, credit progression rates. Supports 文化庁's gender equity commitments (5th Basic Plan on Gender Equality Promotion includes cultural industry labor).

3. **Regional & studio-tier inequality** — Credit visibility by Tokyo vs. regional studios, major vs. independent producers. Relevant to regional revitalization policy (地域創生) and support for non-Tokyo production centers.

4. **Succession & career pipeline** — "Next-generation animator identification" via network centrality and role progression. Aligns with cultural sustainability objectives (若手育成).

**Assumed concerns**:
- Individual-level exposure (fear of animator privacy violation or use of research for surveillance-like hiring)
- Pressure to make normative recommendations ("Fix gender gap by doing X")
- Coordination with labor ministry (turf conflict; MEXT vs. MHLW responsibilities)

**Recommended approach**:
- **Explicit privacy framing**: Public credit = published data; no private interviews; aggregated-level findings only (no individual animator profiling)
- Lead with **structural finding** (inequality exists), defer **prescriptive recommendation** (how to fix) to policy deliberation
- Offer short Policy Brief (2 pages) + Technical Appendix (demographic analysis, method notes) — separate documents for different audiences
- Propose formal research exchange (文化庁研究会への招聘) with methodology transparency

### 1.3 Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare (MHLW / 厚生労働省)
#### Labour Standards Bureau + Employment Equality Division
*Contact via: Freelance & Gig Worker Division (フリーランス・ギグワーカー課) [est. 2024] and Labour Standards Division (労働基準課)*

**Jurisdiction & Policy Cycles**:
- Freelancer Protection Act (フリーランス保護新法, 2024 enforcement)
- Animation labor as case study for gig-economy labor standards
- Working hours, compensation transparency, contract fairness
- Occupational safety & well-being in creative industries

**Typical staffing**:
- Division director (課長)
- Labour standards inspectors / policy advisors
- Gig economy research unit (relatively new, small team)

**Policy cycles**:
- Freelance Protection Act implementation review (2025–2026, full schedule 2026 Q4–Q1 planning)
- Industry working group formation (ongoing; animation labor prioritized by advocacy groups)
- Labour standards guidance updates (annual, but gig economy guidance = priority for 2026)

**Assumed interest in Animetor Eval**:

1. **Hidden labor conditions** — Credit-based reconstruction of work timing, collaboration intensity, studio-hopping patterns. Indirectly reveals occupational hazard exposure (tight production schedules, unstable income) without relying on sensitive interviews.

2. **Wage-setting disparities** — Production scale (AKM fixed effect analysis) vs. actual compensation inequality by role/experience. Supports argument that **observable work scope ≠ compensation fairness**, motivating new transparency standards.

3. **Occupational health risks** — Next-year credit visibility loss may correlate with health outcomes, burnout-driven exits. Hygienic surveillance: "which studio configurations predict staff exit risk?" (for MHLW guidance to industry).

4. **Union representation & collective bargaining** — Network analysis of studio-level labor organizing potential (degree distribution, small-world properties suggest organizing difficulty in fragmented freelance network). Relevant to MHLW's labor organizing support initiatives.

**Assumed concerns**:
- Individual compensation data (MHLW may want micro-level wage validation; animetor has no wage data)
- Risk of research being used to **justify** low wages ("anime workers are structurally less central, so lower pay is fair")
- Pressure to recommend union enforcement or prosecution (beyond research scope)

**Recommended approach**:
- **Explicit data boundary**: "This analysis uses public credit data only. We do not collect wages, do not infer wages, do not make wage recommendations."
- Lead with **structural inequality quantification** (opportunity disparities are independent of wage data)
- Offer specific brief: "Credit visibility loss as occupational health proxy" + method limitations
- Propose **no formal recommendation** on enforcement; instead offer MHLW the option to commission follow-up survey (joint research with labor ministry).

- Creative sector reports (ad hoc, typically Q1–Q2)

Relevant to IP strategy: "Where does anime IP value actually originate?" (individual animator vs. studio vs. production committee). Animetor's network view suggests answer: **distributed across stable collaborator networks**.

2. **Talent retention as IP asset** — Framed as "IP ecosystem stability." If animators leave (credit visibility loss), the associated IP production capacity declines. Portfolio risk assessment.

3. **International competitiveness** — Quantified evidence that anime production is geographically concentrated (Tokyo studios), organizationally fragmented (many small independent studios). Relevant to IP export strategy: "Do we have sufficient capacity to serve global demand?" (Resource bottleneck identification).

**Assumed concerns**:

- **Narrow framing**: "Anime IP ecosystem stability monitoring tool" (1-page brief)
- Propose **annual structural metric dashboard**: production concentration, studio diversity, animator cohort progression
- Position as **complement to sales data** (not replacement), for strategic planning
- Offer light-touch briefing (30 min) to IP strategy committee, no formal report required initially

## 2. Contact Sequencing Strategy (Risk Gradient)

Ministries vary in institutional risk, political sensitivity, and coordination requirements. Recommended sequence (low to high institutional friction):

### Phase 1: Low-friction windows (Q2–Q3 2026, parallel)
1. **METI Content Industry Division** — purely economic/structural interest; lowest political salience
2. **Cabinet Office IP Strategy** — narrow request scope; coordination-focused, not advocacy-focused

**Rationale**: Establish research credibility with "safe" audiences before addressing labor/gender politics.

### Phase 2: Medium-friction window (Q3 2026, after Phase 1 traction)
3. **MEXT Media Arts Division** — cultural policy interest; some political sensitivity around gender, but established policy framework

**Prerequisite**: Positive signal from METI (reference, feedback)

**Timing rationale**: 文化庁 planning for 5-year cultural plan (2027–2032 cycle) begins Q4 2026; input needed by Q3.

### Phase 3: High-friction window (Q4 2026–Q1 2027, after Phase 1–2 confidence-building)
4. **MHLW Labour Standards** — sensitive to labor organizing, firm retaliation, individual privacy; requires careful framing

**Prerequisites**:
- Positive signals from METI, MEXT (institutional credibility)
- Explicit COI disclosure completed (startup context known and vetted by other ministries)
- Mature policy brief on labor dimensions

## 3. Conflict of Interest (COI) Disclosure Framework

### 3.1 Context: Startup Status & Personal Interest

Animetor Eval is operated by the researcher as an **independent startup project** (not thesis-bound; faculty advisor not involved). The project has multiple, potentially intersecting impact pathways:

| Pathway | Focus | Personal stake | Duration |
|---------|-------|-----------------|----------|
| **Publication** | Economics/network science journals | Academic reputation, disciplinary contribution | ongoing (2027+) |
| **Policy** | Government research transfer | Institutional legitimacy, policy influence | 2026–2028 |
| **Business** | B2C/B2B SaaS (animator-facing tools, aggregate analytics for studios) | Revenue, business viability | 2026–2027+ |

**Policy track weighting**: 32% of projected impact (STANCE.md §2). Business track weighting: 9%.

**Inherent tension**: Policy stakeholders (government) and business stakeholders (studios, platforms) may have conflicting interests. Government's labor-first priorities may clash with studios' HR cost-minimization goals. If business track prioritizes studio customers, policy recommendations may be compromised.

### 3.2 Disclosure Statement (Template for Each Ministry)

**To be delivered orally in first contact + attached to every brief:**

---

**CONFLICT OF INTEREST DISCLOSURE — Animetor Eval Project**

*Researcher: [NAME]  
Date: [Contact Date]  
Ministry: [METI / MEXT / MHLW / Cabinet Office]*

The Animetor Eval project is operated by the researcher as an independent startup (not affiliated with university; no thesis-binding). The project pursues three impact pathways concurrently:

1. **Publication track** (59% weight): Academic papers in economics/information science venues
2. **Policy track** (32% weight): Government research transfer; policy brief provision
3. **Business track** (9% weight): SaaS product development for aggregated labor market data (B2C animator-facing; B2B studio analytics)

**Potential conflicts relevant to your ministry**:

- **METI**: If business track develops studio-facing analytics products, animetor may gain competitive advantage from early METI data access or legitimacy. *Mitigation*: Studio product is exclusively **anonymized, aggregated** (no individual animator data exposure); business roadmap does not include executive hiring recommendations or personnel-level analytics. METI's data requests will not influence business product design.

- **MEXT**: If business track targets studio HR customers, incentive may exist to soften findings on gender/opportunity inequality (studios may resist features that highlight wage gaps). *Mitigation*: findings on inequality are independently verified (academic publication pending); policy brief reflects best scientific evidence regardless of business viability. Gender analysis is not optional/suppressed for commercial reasons.

- **MHLW**: If business track develops tools to monitor studio-level labor conditions, risk exists of tools being misused by studios to identify and suppress labor organizers. *Mitigation*: Studio product is strictly **studio-aggregate only** (no individual-level data); no features exist for intra-studio surveillance. Animetor will not knowingly sell to studios with history of union-busting or retaliation.

- **Cabinet Office**: IP strategy value may be overstated if business scale is limited (i.e., if startup fails, IP ecosystem monitoring capacity is lost). *Mitigation*: Research transfer is independent of business viability; policy brief can be maintained by government data team if startup shuts down.

**Proactive steps taken**:
- Startup legal entity structure documented ([DETAILS: under preparation per TASK_CARDS/31_business/01_startup_form](url not specified; will be provided upon request))
- Business roadmap explicitly excludes individual-level analytics and hiring recommendations
- Publication strategy is committed independently (RIETI DP + Labour Economics track); business product has no publication-blocking authority
- All three tracks report outputs publicly; no proprietary blackout on methodology or findings
- JAniCA (animator union) standing invite as policy advisor; union feedback incorporated into policy brief iteration

**Mitigation governance**:
- Annual conflict audit: three-track alignment review (publication findings vs. business product features vs. policy brief recommendations)
- External ethics review planned for high-impact publications (RIETI DP, Labour Economics)

*Researcher signature: [DATE, NAME]*

## 4. Report-to-Window Matching

Each ministry has distinct data / finding priorities. The Animetor Eval v3 report system (as of 2026-05) includes:

- **3 executive briefs** (Policy, HR, Business)
- **15 technical reports** (detailed analysis modules)
- **8 visualization primitives**

The following table maps reports to ministry interests:

| Ministry | Primary Interest | Matched Reports | Brief format |
|----------|------------------|-----------------|--------------|
| **METI** | Production capacity, market concentration, studio network topology | Policy Brief (production scale section) + Technical Report T-02 (studio collaboration density) + T-09 (production HHI) | 2-page brief (1000 words) + 5-page technical appendix (method notes) |
| **MEXT** | Gender/demographic inequality, role progression, regional disparity | Policy Brief (all sections) + T-05 (gender-differentiated role assignment) + T-10 (demographic opportunity structure) + T-14 (regional studio tier effects) | 2-page brief + 8-page cultural policy context + visualization set |
| **MHLW** | Labor stability (credit visibility loss), occupational health proxy, union organizing structure | Custom 3-page brief (labor-specific findings) + T-03 (credit visibility loss by studio) + T-06 (next-year retention prediction) + network resilience metrics | 3-page brief + technical notes on data limitations (no wage inference) |
| **Cabinet Office** | Production ecosystem monitoring, IP asset stability, strategic resource availability | 1-page "IP Ecosystem Scorecard" (6-month snapshot) + T-08 (studio diversity index) + T-12 (animator cohort progression) | 1-page dashboard + rolling annual metrics |

**Brief format notes**:
- METI: straightforward data; method-gate focus on uncertainty bounds
- MEXT: narrative emphasis on fairness implications; dual language (JA/EN) required
- MHLW: explicit data limitations (no wage inference, no individual-level data); labor-first framing essential
- Cabinet Office: minimal text; visual dashboard preferred; metric definitions provided separately

## 6. Meeting Format & Deliverables

### 6.1 First Meeting: 30–60 minutes

**Agenda**:
1. (5 min) Introduction: your research background, project timeline
2. (15 min) Presentation of 2-page brief + key findings
3. (20 min) Q&A: methodology, data limitations, relevance to ministry priorities
4. (5 min) Next steps: follow-up engagement options

**Preparation**:
- Print 1-page brief summary (bullet points)
- Prepare 3–5 sample visualizations (charts, not dense tables)
- Anticipate 2–3 deepest questions ministry is likely to ask (see section 1 "Assumed concerns")
- Do NOT offer formal recommendation in this meeting (only in Interpretation section of written brief, if at all)

### 6.2 Follow-up Engagement Options (to propose at end of meeting)

**Option A: Research briefing series** (3–4 meetings over 6 months)
- Q1: Production structure findings
- Q2: Labor dynamics findings  
- Q3: Policy implications (if ministry requests)

**Option B: Formal research collaboration**
- Co-authored policy research proposal
- METI/MEXT research fund application (joint PI)
- Joint press release on findings

**Option C: Policy advisory role**
- Formal membership in ministry working group (if available)
- Written testimony for policy deliberation (if appropriate)
- Data consultation on government survey design

**Option D: Standalone delivery**
- One-time policy brief (done; no further engagement)
- Optional: annual update (calendar-based or event-triggered)

## 7. Expected Ministry Reactions & Response Strategies

### Scenario A: Positive / Data-Hungry
**Signal**: "This is exactly what we need for [policy initiative]. Can you deliver monthly metrics?"

**Risks**:
- Over-commitment of research capacity
- Pressure to serve specific political agenda
- Mission creep (reporting becomes prioritized over analysis)

**Response strategy**:
- Propose **quarterly** (not monthly) metrics (realistic for data quality, re-scraping constraints)
- Written SLA specifying metric definitions, update frequency, data freshness tolerance
- Reserve right to flag data quality issues without deadline pressure

---

### Scenario B: Neutral / Feasibility Questions
**Signal**: "Interesting, but do you have capacity to deliver? What if studios stop publishing credits?"

**Risks**:
- Perception of research as unreliable / over-ambitious
- Low institutional priority (filed as low-effort, low-value)

**Response strategy**:
- Provide 3-year sustainability plan (including alternative data sources, fallback metrics)
- Offer pilot phase: "Start with 1 report, 6-month evaluation before scaling"
- Emphasize independence: "Unlike consulting, our timeline doesn't depend on ministry funding"

---

### Scenario C: Skeptical / Turf Protection
**Signal**: "This overlaps with our existing survey. Why should we partner with you?"

**Risks**:
- Ministry perceives animetor as threat to existing contractor relationships
- Government internally protects budget allocation to incumbent research organizations

**Response strategy**:
- Position as **complement**, not replacement: "Validates your survey with independent data; increases credibility"
- Propose **survey augmentation**, not replacement: "Our metrics fit into your existing RFP as supplementary module"
- Offer data access to incumbent contractors (transparency reduces turf threat)

---

### Scenario D: Cautious / Privacy Concerns
**Signal**: "We're interested, but concerned about animator privacy. How do you handle individual data?"

**Risks**:
- Ministry blocks engagement due to privacy regulation (APPI)
- Requirement for formal consent from all animators (infeasible)

**Response strategy**:
- Reiterate: "All data is already public (published in credits). We aggregate only; no individual animator identification in reports."
- Offer **aggregation guarantee** in writing: "No report contains identifiable person-level metrics; all findings are studio-level or population-level."
- If ministry still concerned, offer **de-identified research dataset** (remove names, dates; provide only statistical summaries)

---

### Scenario E: Challenging / Labor Union Questions
**Signal**: "Will animators see this research? Will it be used against them (for wage-setting)?"

**Risks**:
- JAniCA or individual animator criticism (research seen as management-sided)
- Labor ministry directive to cease publication if concerns escalate

**Response strategy**:
- **Proactive JAniCA engagement** (before ministry contact if possible): brief union leadership, incorporate feedback into findings and framing
- Written statement: "This research is designed to **strengthen animator labor power** (visible evidence of structural inequality), not weaken it. We will not suppress findings if they show gender/opportunity gaps."
- Propose joint publication: "JAniCA can request co-authorship or joint press release if research aligns with union priorities"

## 8. Long-term Ministry Engagement Model (Post-Initial Contact)

If ministries engage beyond initial briefing, recommend the following governance:

### 8.1 Quarterly Metric Dashboard (Optional Service)
- 6 structural KPIs (studio concentration, animator retention, role diversity, etc.)
- 1-page summary + 2-page detailed method notes
- Delivered Q1, Q3 (tied to government fiscal year, not continuous)
- Cost-sharing: Ministry bears data-update costs (scraping, processing); animetor provides analysis

### 8.2 Annual Policy Brief Update
- Refresh findings with latest data (1-year lag from current date)
- Incorporate ministry feedback / policy changes
- Maintain consistent methodology (allow comparability across years)

### 8.3 Research Collaboration Option
- If ministry has specific research question, animetor can propose co-designed study
- Examples:
  - METI: "Does anime production scale correlate with export revenue?" (anometor does structure, METI provides trade data)
  - MEXT: "How does animator gender diversity affect production timelines?" (anometor does gender/role analysis, MEXT surveys time-to-completion)
  - MHLW: "Animator credit loss rate as occupational health proxy" (anometor provides data, MHLW conducts health survey validation)

### 8.4 Governance & Conflicts Review (Annual)
- August: Animetor leadership reviews business/policy/publication track alignment
- October: If conflicts detected, disclose to all engaged ministries + propose mitigation
- Example trigger: "Business track is now targeting studio HR products; this changes COI calculus"

## 9. Restricted Commitments (What NOT to Offer)

To maintain research integrity and labor-first framing, explicitly do **not** offer:

1. **Individual animator scoring for government use** — "Provide scores suitable for certification / subsidy allocation / hiring recommendation"
   - Violates hard rule H2 (ability framing prohibited)
   - Requires method gates incompatible with public release
   - **Instead**: Offer studio-aggregate or population-aggregate metrics only

2. **Studio-specific confidential reports** — "Prepare a custom analysis for Studio X's labor costs"
   - Creates direct client relationship with studios (conflicts with labor-first stance)
   - Would require fee structure; makes business model dependent on studio goodwill
   - **Instead**: Offer aggregate "studio tier benchmarks" available to all studios equally

3. **Policy recommendation without evidence** — "Here's what METI should do about anime production subsidies"
   - Violates REPORT_PHILOSOPHY.md rule (Interpretation section must separate from Findings)
   - Overstates research capacity; anometor is not a policy consultant
   - **Instead**: "Here are three policy scenarios and their likely structural consequences (based on modeling, not optimization)"

4. **Real-time intelligence / competitive advantage** — "Alert METI immediately if Studio X's animator retention drops"
   - Creates surveillance-like dynamic; shifts anometor toward government-contractor relationship
   - Breaks publication independence (if government gets news first, research is not novel)
   - **Instead**: Quarterly / annual snapshots only; published findings are public (no confidential intel)

5. **Union-busting support** — Explicit or implicit: "Here's a list of studios with high labor-organizing risk"
   - Even if offered as "studio resilience metrics," misuse is near-certain
   - Violates labor-first stance
   - **Instead**: If MHLW requests this, explicitly decline + explain

## 10. Success Metrics & Evaluation

Engagement success is measured not by "favorable government response" but by structural outputs:

| Metric | Target | Evaluation |
|--------|--------|------------|
| **Ministry-initiated follow-up** | ≥2 of 4 ministries contact anometor with substantive question within 6 months | Indicates research relevance |
| **Institutional acknowledgment** | ≥1 ministry cites anometor findings in policy document (Council report, subsidy program RFP, etc.) by end 2026 | Institutional legitimacy |
